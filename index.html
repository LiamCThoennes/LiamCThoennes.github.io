Everything is Political

The Moral Imperative of Public Speech in a Democracy
Written by Liam C Thoennes

This is for every influencer, every celebrity, every athlete who has ever said, "I'm not political."

This paper is an argument against that excuse. It is the case for why, in a democracy teetering on the brink, that statement is not just a falsehood; it is a cowardly and complicit act. To understand why, we must first dismantle the myth they hide behind: the idea of a separate "private sphere." This myth, first powerfully dismantled by second-wave feminists who declared that "the personal is political," was never a reality; it was an artificial wall built to protect the powerful and devalue the experiences of the marginalized.

From there, we will take a brief global tour to prove this reality is universal, using three analytical spectrums to show how politics plays out everywhere, from Japan to Poland, from Germany to the UK.

This journey will prove that the calls to "shut up and entertain" are not good-faith criticisms. They are a tactic used by those who benefit from the status quo, because if your voice didn't have power, they wouldn't be so threatened by it. And it will lead to an undeniable conclusion: for anyone with a public platform, the only choice is not whether to be political, but which side you are on. Will you choose the comfortable silence that protects the powerful, or the difficult courage that fights for the powerless?

Now that you've seen the diagram. On the surface, it's simple: two circles, Public and Private. But the central argument of this paper, and the reality of our modern world, lies in that messy, overlapping space in the middle. The idea of a truly "private" sphere has always been a convenient fiction.

That fiction was a privilege, mostly for those who benefited from the system and could afford to say, "keep politics out of it." It allowed society to ignore the injustices that happen behind closed doors. But that wall has been shattered.

This brings us to the center of the diagram, the space where we all now live: The Political Self. This is the new reality, supercharged by technology and social media, where every action is a potential statement and every personal choice can have public consequences. It's the space where we rightly judge a person's "private" character. How they treat people when they think no one is watching is a direct reflection of their character and their fitness to hold our public trust.

For a regular citizen, this reality can be complex. For a public figure with a platform, it is an inescapable condition of their existence within a democracy. Every tweet is a press release. Every Instagram story is a political performance. In this space, neutrality is an illusion.

This is the new stage on which every public figure now lives. To understand the rules of this stage, we must first look at how different cultures around the world watch their political performers.

As the spectrum above illustrates, the "rules" for public life are not universal. The pressure a public figure feels in France, where privacy is culturally and legally framed as a component of human dignity, is fundamentally different from the pressure in the UK or the US, where a public figure's private life is seen as a crucial test of their public character.

Furthermore, this clean spectrum hides a messy reality, particularly in a hyper-polarized nation like the United States. Here, scrutiny is often used as a political weapon, not a neutral tool for accountability. Its impact becomes tribal: a scandal that would destroy one leader is dismissed by the supporters of another as a "witch hunt." We will explore this American dynamic in greater detail later.

For now, what's important is understanding that this intense, often contradictory pressure is the stage on which every public figure must operate. It forces them to make a choice. Their actions, their words, and even their silence become a conscious performance.

A performance defined by the conscious choice behind every public action: the decision to kneel, to tweet, or to stay silent. Every one of these choices ultimately serves one of two opposing goals: to uphold the status quo, or to demand fundamental change.

This spectrum reveals that the performance of a public figure is a strategic choice, heavily influenced by their culture. Audiences and fanbases now often demand that their public figures take a stand, making the quiet conformity of the past a risky performance in its own right.

And once again, this clean spectrum hides a messy truth. Not all confrontational advocacy is treated equally. A public figure campaigning for a popular cause like free school meals might be celebrated as a national hero. But another figure challenging a more sacred institution, like the police or the military, may be punished and cast out. The consequences of the performance are not just about what you say, but who you are and what you dare to question.

This dynamic, where public figures perform and the public watches, inevitably leads to the final act of our story. When the performance of leaders is not enough, or when their actions spark outrage, the people themselves take the stage. This is where we see how different cultures express their collective will.

The Spectrum of Collective Action

This final spectrum tells the ultimate truth of any democracy: the people have the final say. It reveals a clear divide between the Symbolic Mass Protest seen in South Korea, where the goal is to create overwhelming moral pressure, and the Direct Disruption of France and Poland, where the goal is to paralyze the state and force a crisis. In the middle sits the Hybrid model of the United States, where movements strategically blend both tactics.

But like the other spectrums, this one is not static. The rise of social media has become a powerful accelerant for all forms of collective action. It allows symbolic protests to gain global visibility in an instant and helps activists coordinate complex, disruptive actions with unprecedented speed and efficiency. The line between online outrage and offline action has never been thinner.

And once again, this clean model hides a difficult truth. No tactic is guaranteed to succeed. A massive, peaceful protest can be ignored by a government that does not care about public opinion. A disruptive strike can backfire, alienating potential allies and inviting a brutal crackdown from the state. The choice of how to protest is always a high-stakes gamble, dependent on the specific political context and the willingness of the powerful to listen.

Together, these three spectrums tell the full story of how politics plays out in modern democracies. This was the goal: to show the three-act drama of public life.

We began by setting the stage, analyzing the intense pressure of public scrutiny that all figures face. This pressure forces a response, leading us to the conscious performance of public life, where every action is a choice between upholding the status quo or demanding change. And when that performance is not enough, the story culminates with the people themselves taking the stage, choosing to make their statement through symbolic protest or direct disruption.

They show us that the relationship between public scrutiny, individual performance, and collective action is a dynamic and ever-changing dance. There is no single "correct" way to be political; each culture finds its own rhythm, its own methods of challenge and response. They prove that we are never bound to do things one way, and that sometimes, we must change it up.

Now, let's bring this framework home and apply it to the most complex and volatile case study of all: the United States.

The American Case Study: A Nation of Extremes

Now that we have a global framework, we can turn our focus to the most complex and volatile case study: the United States. On every spectrum, America occupies a position of unique intensity. It is a political pressure cooker, combining a commercialized system of scrutiny with a branded performance of advocacy and a historical legacy of hybrid protest. This unique combination creates an environment with unparalleled stakes and a profound responsibility for the public figures who operate within it.

The Pressure Cooker of Scrutiny: A System That Forces a Choice

The US position on the "Intense Scrutiny" end of the spectrum is the result of two powerful systems working in tandem. First, the First Amendment's legal protection of a free press created the space for investigation. Second, the rise of a commercialized, 24/7 news cycle in the late 20th century created the financial incentive. Scandal sells, and character attacks became a professionalized tool of political warfare. This means that for a public figure in America, their life will be politicized whether they want it to be or not. The idea of being an "apolitical" celebrity is a fantasy, as the media and political operatives will scrutinize their personal life and assign a political meaning to it. This reality leaves a public figure with only one real choice: either allow their narrative to be defined for them by others, or seize control of it by speaking their own values clearly and forcefully.

The Marketplace of Dissent: Advocacy as Brand

The American tradition of "Confrontational Advocacy" is deeply tied to the nation's core myth of the rugged individual, but in the modern era, it has fused with capitalism and celebrity branding. A political stance is no longer just a belief; it is a performance of identity that can be integrated into a personal brand. This has created a commercial marketplace for advocacy, where taking a stand can be both a moral statement and a powerful business decision. Because of this, the public has become highly attuned to authenticity, raising the stakes for those with a platform. It creates a moral imperative to move beyond "safe" or "brand-friendly" stances and use their influence to advocate for issues that carry real risk. The case of Colin Kaepernick is the ultimate proof: he was punished because his protest was too authentic for the NFL's commercial brand, revealing the line between marketable advocacy and genuine dissent.

The Burden of History: The Imperative to Protest

The American "Hybrid" model of collective action was born from historical necessity. For most of American history, marginalized groups were systematically denied access to formal political power and could not rely on voting or the courts alone. This forced them to develop a two-pronged approach: symbolic protest (like the March on Washington) to appeal to the conscience of the nation, and direct disruption (like the Montgomery Bus Boycott) to create a crisis that the powerful could not ignore. This history creates a powerful cultural script and a moral precedent. When public figures today see injustices that mirror these historical struggles, they are not just witnessing a news event; they are being called to fulfill their role in this tradition. Their massive platform allows them to be the "symbolic" part of the protest on a global scale, amplifying the message of the "disruptive" actions happening on the streets. To stay silent is to betray this legacy and to abandon the very tools that have historically been used to bend the arc of the moral universe toward justice.

This unique combination of intense scrutiny, expected advocacy, and a powerful history of protest creates a political environment unlike any other. The American public figure possesses a platform of unparalleled influence, a stage far different from the conformity of Japan or the consensus guarding of Germany. The American system does not just allow for confrontational advocacy; it has culturally and commercially integrated it, making it a core part of public identity.

But this creates the ultimate moral conundrum. The very system that grants these figures immense fame and wealth is often the same system that requires fundamental change. To genuinely advocate for justice means turning around and challenging the institutions, the business connections, and even the fanbases that made you successful. It is not a simple choice. It is a difficult, often career-threatening tightrope walk between comfort and conscience.

And in a moment of democratic crisis, navigating this difficulty is no longer an option; it is the central challenge. This brings us to the final, most urgent part of our argument: the moral imperative to speak in the face of injustice, no matter the cost.

The Moral Imperative: Why Silence is Not an Option

Understanding the global context and the unique American dynamic is not enough. The purpose of this entire analysis is to arrive at a powerful and uncomfortable truth: in a democracy, especially one under stress, the act of speaking out is not just a right; it is a moral duty for those with a platform. To understand why, we must first deconstruct the primary argument used to enforce silence.

Deconstructing the "Shut Up and Entertain" Counterargument

The argument that public figures should stay out of politics is a powerful rhetorical tool used to delegitimize dissent and protect the status quo. It relies on several key fallacies that are not just logically flawed, but morally corrupt.

The Appeal to Expertise ("You're not a politician!")

The Tactic: This frames the public figure as an unqualified amateur stepping into a professional domain.

Why It's Morally Wrong: This is an anti-democratic attempt to create a new class system for speech, where only a select few "experts" are allowed to have a valid opinion. It is a form of gatekeeping designed to silence citizens and protect the powerful from criticism. In a democracy, citizenship itself is the only qualification required to speak on matters of justice.

The Accusation of Hypocrisy ("You're a rich celebrity/streamer!")

The Tactic: Critics will often point to a celebrity's wealth or lifestyle to accuse them of hypocrisy.

Why It's Morally Wrong: This is a deliberate act of deflection that protects the injustice by attacking the messenger. It is a moral poison that suggests only perfect saints have the right to point out a sin. By shifting the focus from the issue at hand (like systemic poverty) to the perceived flaws of the speaker, it allows the original injustice to continue unchallenged.

The "Distraction" Frame ("You're ruining the game!")

The Tactic: In sports and entertainment, activism is often framed as a "distraction" from the event's primary purpose.

Why It's Morally Wrong: This is the most insidious fallacy. It is morally wrong because it attempts to dehumanize the public figure, reducing them from a citizen with a conscience to a product for consumption. It argues that the audience's temporary comfort is more important than another person's life or liberty. It is a demand for apathy, a command to ignore the burning world outside the stadium walls for the sake of the game.

The Kaepernick Precedent: A Warning Shot

After deconstructing the bad-faith arguments for silence, a single, terrifying question remains: Why should anyone risk their career to speak out?

The case of Colin Kaepernick provides the answer by showing the true cost of genuine dissent. In 2016, he began kneeling during the national anthem to protest police brutality and systemic racism. The backlash was not a misunderstanding; it was a calculated campaign of misdirection. Critics, from the NFL front office to the White House, deliberately reframed his protest as an act of disrespect towards the flag and the military, shifting the conversation away from racial injustice and onto a false debate about patriotism.

The NFL, calling him a "distraction," effectively blackballed him from the league. His blackballing from the NFL was not a consequence of his failure; it was a punishment for his power. It was a warning shot fired by the status quo to anyone else who dares to step out of line.

The Moral Calculus of Silence

So why take that risk? The answer lies in a simple, brutal truth that echoes through history: silence in the face of injustice is complicity. It is not a neutral act; it is an active choice that enables oppression.

The Unraveling of Constitutional Norms: An Urgent Call to Action

This brings us to the final, brutal truth of why this paper exists. The moral choice to speak is no longer a theoretical exercise; it has become an urgent necessity. In the contemporary United States, the negotiation between public and private has given way to a systemic assault on the foundational principles of the republic. The events of recent years are not just warning signs; they are the fire itself, a clear and present danger to the fabric of American society.

What does it mean when our system of justice becomes a shield for the powerful instead of a sword for the victim? The handling of the Jeffrey Epstein case provides the answer. The recent release of documents explicitly naming powerful individuals like Donald Trump has been met with a deafening silence from prosecutors. This inaction, contrasted with the aggressive pursuit of ordinary citizens, is a grotesque violation of the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of "equal protection of the laws." When Florida Attorney General Pam Bondi can delay the release of these documents and redact them to protect the accused, it is not a bureaucratic hiccup. It is a functional obstruction of justice. This is not justice; it is a mockery of it.

The Weaponization of Executive Power

The American presidency was never meant to be a throne, but it is being treated as one. The attempt to overturn the 2020 election and the subsequent incitement of the January 6th insurrection was a direct assault on the peaceful transfer of power. The use of pardons to reward loyalty and protect allies from investigation, the reported financial entanglements with foreign powers like the UAE in violation of the Emoluments Clause, and threats of unilateral military action against countries like Venezuela all showcase a flagrant disregard for constitutional limits. This is the transformation of a public trust into a tool for personal enrichment and autocratic ambition.

Conclusion: The Democratic Imperative

We have seen the evidence of a system unraveling. We have seen the foundational principles of the republic, from due process to equal protection, treated not as sacred duties, but as inconvenient obstacles.

In the face of this, the argument to "shut up and entertain" is revealed for what it truly is. It is not a good faith critique. It is an instruction to be a silent witness to the fire. It is a demand that you abdicate your role as a citizen, that you cash in your influence for the comfort of complicity while the very society that granted you a platform burns.

This is a call to action.

To every celebrity, every athlete, every artist, every influencer who has received power and benefit from this system: it is now time to pay your debt. This is not an abstract task. It is a call to protect your neighbors. It is a demand to defend the very systems of law, morals, and ethics that are supposed to hold this nation together.

But this burden does not belong to them alone. It belongs to all of us. We must all be outraged. We must all be vocal. The work of democracy is not a spectator sport. In this moment of peril, silence is consent. Our voices, together, are the last, best line of defense.

It is time to use them.
